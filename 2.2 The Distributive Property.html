2.2 The Distributive Property

The Distributive Property

The distributive property is given in the box below. It is an incredibly useful tool to manipulate algebraic expressions.

The Distributive Property:

\(a(b+c)=a\cdot b+a\cdot c\)

\(a(b-c)=a\cdot b-a\cdot c\)

Before we focus on using the distributive property with algebraic expressions, it is useful to point out that the idea of the distributive property is something that you are already familiar with.

Imagine that you take a temporary job working for a vendor at the state fair. You will work for 2 days and are going to earn $15 for each hour that you work. Does it matter if the vendor pays you for your hours at the end of each day or if they wait until the end of both days to pay you? Your experience most likely leads you to correctly says that it doesn't matter; the amount you will get paid is the same either way. That's because you already know about the distributive property!

Let's say you work 7 hours on the first day and 9 hours on the second day. If the vendor waits until the end of the two days to calculate your pay, the calculation would look like this:

\("total earnings "\)

\(=($15" per hour")(" day 1 hours " + " day 2 hours ") \)

\(=($15 " per hour")(7 " hours " + " "9 " hours ")\)

\(=($15 " per hour")(16 " hours")\)

\(=$240\)

If instead you get paid at the end of each day, then your total earnings would be calculated as

\("total earnings " \)

\(= " day 1 earnings " + " day 2 earnings "\)

\(= ($15 " per hour")(7 " hours")+($15 " per hour")(9 " hours") \)

\(= $105+$135\)

\(=$240\)

This is exactly the distributive property!

\(a(b+c)=ab+ac\)

\(15(7+9)=15(7)+15(9)\)

Distributing Binomials

If we want to multiply \((3x-2)(5x+4)\)we can use the distributive property. We will distribute the\((3x-2)\) to both the\(5x\) and the 4.

\((3x-2)(5x+4)\)

\(=(3x-2)(5x)+(3x+2)(4)\)

\(=5x(3x-2)+4(3x+2)\)

\(=(5x)(3x)-(5x)(2)+(4)(3x)+4(2)\)

\(=(5)(3)x*x-(5)(2)x+(4)(3)x+4(2)\)

\(=15x^2-10x+12x+8\)

\(=15x^2+2x+8\)

When multiplying two binomials, a process called the FOIL method is sometimes used to find the product. It is called FOIL because we multiply the First terms, the Outer terms, the Inner terms, and then the Last terms of each binomial.

The FOIL method arises out of the distributive property. We are simply multiplying each term of the first binomial by each term of the second binomial, and then combining like terms.

Given two binomials, then, we can use FOIL to simplify the expression by following these steps.

  1. Multiply the FIRST terms of each binomial.
  2. Multiply the OUTER terms of the binomials.
  3. Multiply the INNER terms of the binomials.
  4. Multiply the LAST terms of each binomial.
  5. Add the products.
  6. Combine like terms and simplify.

Example

Use the FOIL method to find the product: \((2x - 9)(3x + 4)\)

Solution

The product of the first terms is \((2x)(3x) = 6x^2\)

The product of the outer terms is \((2x)(4) = 8x\)

The product of the inner terms is \((-9)(3x) = -27x\)

The product of the last terms is \((-9)(4) = -36\)

\(6x^2+8x-27x-36\)

Add the products.

\(6x^2-19x-36\)

Combine like terms.

Difference of Squares

A notable pattern occurs when we multiply a binomial by another binomial with the same terms but the opposite sign. Binomial pairs of this type are called conjugate pairs. Let’s see what happens when we multiply conjugate pairsusing the FOIL method.

\((x + 4)(x - 4)\)

=

\(x^2-4x+4x-16\)

=

\(x^2 - 16\)

The middle term is the sum of opposites, in this case -4\(x\) and 4\(x\). The sum is 0\(x\) = 0. So in effect, the middle term drops out, resulting in a difference of squares.

Let’s look at a few more examples.

\((x + 5)(x - 5)\)

=

\(x^2-5x+5x-25=x^2-25\)

\((x + 11)(x - 11)\)

=

\(x^2-11x+11x-121=x^2-121\)

\((2x + 3)(2x - 3)\)

=

\(4x^2-6x+6x-9=4x^2-9\)

Noticing the pattern of the products, we can write a general formula and use it as a 'shortcut' when multiplying conjugate pairs.

Difference of Squares

For real numbersa andb, \((a+b)(a-b)=a^2-b^2\)
