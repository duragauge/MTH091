In the previous section, we discussed inverse operations, and how to perform an operation on both sides of an equation in order to solve it.

Now we need to talk about equations in which multiple operations are required in order to solve.

Before we discuss the theory behind solving multi-step equations, we need to make sure we know the correct order of operations. This was covered in detail in the previous chapter, but here's a quick review:

Order of Operations

  1. Perform any operations within groupings. (If there are multiple operations within a grouping, use the following steps when performing them.)
  2. Apply any exponents. (Note: if there is a root, it would be applied at this stage as well)
  3. Apply any multiplication or division, moving from left to right.
  4. Apply any addition or subtraction, moving from left to right.


Not only is it important to be able to perform operations in the correct order -- it's also important to be able to identify the operations that are taking place in an equation, in the correct order.

Example 1

Identify, in the correct order, the operations happening to the variable on the left side of each equation.

(a) \(5x - 48 = -23\) (b) \((3n+2)/7 = 5\) (c) \((p-16)^2 + 9 = 73\)

Answers

(a) The left side of the equation is the expression \(5x-48\)

Since multiplication happens before subtraction, we know that the operations happening to the variable are:

  1. Multiply by 5
  2. Subtract 48

(b) The expression on the left side is \((3n+2)/7\)

Recall that when one or more operations happen in the numerator or denominator of a fraction, that constitutes a grouping. We can enclose the numerator in parentheses to see this:

\(((3n+2))/7\)

Therefore, the operations happening to the variable are:

  1. Multiply by 3
  2. Add 2
  3. Divide by 7

(c) The expression on the left side is \((p-16)^2 + 9\)

The operations happening to the variable are:

  1. Subtract 16
  2. Exponent of 2
  3. Add 9

As the name implies, a multi-step equation requires that we perform multiple operations in order to find the solution. We discussed inverse operations in the previous section. Now, it's time to learn about inverse processes.

Let's consider a multi-step process in which the steps must be done in a particular order: putting on socks and shoes.

We know that the socks must be put on before the shoes. However, what is the inverse process? In other words, what is the process that is required in order to undo the original actions?

We want to take off our socks and shoes, and because the shoes were the last things to be put on, they are the first things that must be taken off. Therefore, we'll take off our shoes first, and then we'll take off our socks.

This illustrates the notion of an inverse process: In order to undo a multi-step process, the steps must be undone in the reverse order of how they were originally done.

Inverse Actions

\( uarr\)

1. Put on Socks

2. Take off Socks

2. Put on Shoes

1. Take off Shoes

\(->\)


Let's see how that works in the first equation from the previous example:

\(5x - 48 = -23\)

The last operation happening on the left side of the equation is "subtract 48". We'll undo that first, by adding 48. (And, of course, we'll be doing that on both sides of the equation.)

\(5x - 48 = -23\)
\(" "ul(+48) " "" " ul(+48)\)
\(5x" "=" "25\)

Now we can undo the first operation that was happening on the left side, "multiply by 5".

\((5x)/5 = 25/5\)

\(x = 5\)

However, there are more complicated equations, such as \(x^2 + 7x - 12 = 6\), in which the variable appears more than once. In future sections, we will be developing strategies that can be used to solve equations like these.

In the morning, before I go to school or work, as part of my work to get ready for the day, I put on my socks. Then I put on my shoes. At the end of the day, before I go to bed, I have to undo all of that. In order to uno that sequence of actions, we prefer the inverse actions (take off socks/shoes rather than put on socks/shoes), and we do those inverse actions in the reverse order of how the actions were originally done. We put on socks, then shoes. We take off shoes, then socks.
