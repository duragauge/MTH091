5.1 The Cartesian Coordinate System

A Quick History of the Cartesian Coordinate System

According to stories, the French Mathematician Rene Descartes (famous for penning the phrase, "I think therefore I am") invented the modern way of graphing in the 1600s. The cartesian coordinate system that we will explore in this section gets its name from Rene Descartes' last name).

According to the story, while laying in his bed, Descartes was watching a bug crawling around on his ceiling. It occurred to him that he could describe the location of the bug by describing how far it was from each of the two walls forming one of the corners of the ceiling.

We call this graphing grid a cartesian coordinate system or a coordinate plane. A cartesian coordinate system or coordinate plane involves two number lines, each serving as an axis for the coordinate system.

The horizontal axis is typically referred to as the x-axis. The vertical axis is typically referred to as the y-axis.

The point where the two axes meet is called the origin. The two axes together divide the plane into 4 rectangular quarters. We call these the 4 quadrants and refer to them as quadrant I, II, III, and IV.

Graphing Ordered Pairs

To graph on a cartesian coordinate system, we need values for two coordinates: an x-coordinate and a y-coordinate. The x-coordinate tells us a location along the x-axis. The y-coordinate tells us a location along the y-axis. Together, those two coordinates tell us a specific location in the plane.

The point graphed above has an x-coordinate of 2 and a y-coordinate of 3. We refer to the location of this point with the ordered pair (2,3). With an ordered pair, we always list the x-coordinate first and the y-coordinate second.

Ordered Pairs

An ordered pair refers to the location of a point in a Cartesian Coordinate System by describing both the x-coordinate and the y-coordinate in the form: (x-coordinate, y-coordinate).

Example: Use an ordered pair to describe the location of each point labeled in the graph below.

A is the point (1,3). This point is in quadrant I.

B is the point (4,2). This point is in quadrant I.

C is the point (-3,-5). This point is in quadrant III.

E is the point (3,-5). This point is in quadrant IV.

F is the point (-3,0). This point is on the x-axis.

G is the point (0,0). This point is on the origin.

One of the most common mistakes people make with ordered pairs is to list the coordinates in reverse order. So always remember, an ordered pair has the form (x-coordinate, y-coordinate).

Graphing Equations

In addition to graphing specific points like we saw above, graphing can give us a fantastic way to visualize equations and relationships between variables!

Let's start with an example. Here in the United States, we tend to be familiar with the Fahrenheit scale for measuring temperature. In most of the rest of the world, it is more common to see temperature measured in Celsius. As a result, when a US native travels abroad, understanding temperature forecasts can be a challenge. The equation\(F=1.8C+32\) can be used to convert a temperature from Celsius to Fahrenheit. We are going to do a couple of things with this equation.

First, let's just use it a few times.

If the temperature forecast calls for 15 degrees Celsius, we could determine the corresponding temperature in Fahrenheit by using the given formula.

\(F=1.8 \cdot 15+32\)

\(F=27+32\)

\(F=59\)

A temperature of 15 degrees Celsius is equivalent to a temperature of 59 degrees Fahrenheit. If you are headed outside, you might want a light jacket.

What if the temperature was forecast to be -10 degrees Celsius?

\(F=1.8 \cdot (-10)+32\)

\(F=-18+32\)

\(F=14\)

A temperature of -10 degrees Celsius is equivalent to a temperature of 14 degrees Fahrenheit. You better be prepared for subfreezing conditions!
